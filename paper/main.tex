\begin{abstract}
    Federated Learning (FL) facilitates the joint training of machine
    learning models without centralizing sensitive data, making it highly suitable
    for Network Intrusion Detection Systems (NIDS). However, FL is vulnerable to
    inference attacks that may compromise training data privacy and data exposure
    attacks by honest-but-curious servers \cite{ameria2023}. To mitigate these
    risks, privacy-preserving mechanisms such as Differential Privacy (DP) and
    Secure Aggregation (SA) are employed \cite{khraisat2024}. This work presents
    an empirical evaluation of the impact of DP and SA on the performance of
    a federated NIDS using industry-level framework NVIDIA FLARE (NVFlare).

\section{Introduction}

Modern NIDS rely more and more on machine learning (ML) and deep learning (DL),
which require access to large datasets to efficiently and accurately identify
cyber threats \cite{khraisat2024}. Traditional centralized approaches introduce
privacy risks and regulatory challenges due to data concentration and storage
of sensitive information.

FL addresses these issues by enabling distributed model training without sharing
raw data \cite{yurdem2024}. However, FL remains vulnerable to inference
attacks, including membership inference, which can expose whether specific
samples were used during training \cite{ameria2023}. Additionally, FL is vulnerable
to data exposure attacks and datta poisoning attacks by honest-but-curious servers
\cite{he2025}.

To mitigate these risks, additional privacy-preserving mechanisms such as
DP and SA are integrated into FL pipelines \cite{khraisat2024}. DP protects
individual data points by injecting controlled noise, while SA ensures
that the server only observes aggregated model updates.

This paper evaluates the impact of these mechanisms on model performance,
privacy, and computational cost in a realistic federated NIDS setting using
industry-standard framework NVFlare.

\section{Related Work}

Federated Learning has gained significant attention across domains such as
crytical infrastructure, healthcare, and finance due to its privacy-preserving
capabilities \cite{yurdem2024}. In the context of NIDS, FL enables
collaborative training across distributed environments without exposing
sensitive data.

Despite these advantages, FL introduces several challenges, including
vulnerability to inference attacks, communication overhead, and data
heterogeneity \cite{khraisat2024}. In particular, inference attacks pose
a major risk, as adversaries can extract sensitive information from model
updates \cite{ameria2023}.

DP has been widely adopted as a defense mechanism
against such attacks, ensuring that individual data contributions
cannot be easily inferred \cite{owasp2023}. However, the addition of
noise typically degrades model performance.

SA, on the other hand, prevents the central server from
accessing individual client updates by using cryptographic techniques
such as homomorphic encryption \cite{khraisat2024}. While this method
preserves accuracy, it introduces additional computational and
communication overhead.

Recent work has explored privacy-preserving FL for intrusion detection,
but often lacks comprehensive experimental evaluation using large-scale
datasets and industry frameworks \cite{torre2024}. Additionally, there
is limited analysis of the quantitative trade-off between privacy,
performance and computational cost in this context.

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

All experiments were conducted on a single NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPU with 24GB of
memory, a Ryzen 5800x3D CPU and 32GB of RAM. This setup reflects
a high end consumer-grade machine, which is more accessible than
enterprise-grade hardware while still providing sufficient computational
power for training complex models.

For the experiments, NVFlare framework was used to implement the
federated learning pipeline. The framework was runned in a WSL environment
on the same machine, simulating multiple clients for the federated setup.
The same codebase was used for both the centralized and federated experiments
to ensure a fair comparison. The code is available in the following repository:
\url{https://github.com/carandp/FL-NIDS}.

\subsection{Dataset}

The NF-CSE-CIC-IDS2018-v3 dataset was used in the experiments.
The dataset is derived from NF-CSE-CIC-IDS2018, a dataset collected from
a large scale infrastructure deployed in AWS, containing 50 attacker
machines and 5 departments composed of 420 PCs and 30 servers \cite{csecicids2018}
\cite{sharafaldin2018}. This dataset, provides aggregated representations
of network traffic suitable for large-scale intrusion detection \cite{luay2025}.
Due to hardware constraints, a representative subset of the main dataset
was used for training and evaluation.

\subsection{Centralized Baseline}

For the centralized baseline, a Graph Neural Network (GNN) architecture
was adopted, specifically GraphIDS \cite{guerra2025}. GraphIDS is a
self-supervised intrusion detection model designed to learn normal
network behavior directly from data without requiring labeled attacks.
It operates by first using an inductive GNN to generate embeddings
of each network flow, capturing its local topological context within
the communication graph. These embeddings are then processed by a
Transformer-based encoder-decoder structured as a masked autoencoder,
which reconstructs partially hidden representations. Through this
reconstruction task, the model learns both local structural patterns
and global co-occurrence relationships via self-attention. Deviations
from these learned patterns can subsequently be identified as
potential intrusions. The architecture of the training pipeline,
originally proposed by Guerra et al. \cite{guerra2025}, is shown in
\autoref{fig:guerra_model}.

\begin{figure*}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{guerra_pipeline}
    \caption{Overview of the GraphIDS training pipeline (adapted from \cite{guerra2025}, Figure 3).}
    \label{fig:guerra_model}
\end{figure*}

For the centralized baseline, the training process was monitored
using the following performance metrics:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Macro F1-score
    \item Precision-Recall AUC
    \item Training time (seconds)
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Federated Learning Setup}

The centralized model was adapted into a federated setting using
NVFlare, an industry-grade framework designed for scalable
federated learning deployments \cite{roth2024}. The system consists
of multiple clients simulating distributed network environments.
For each job configuration, the best global model is saved and evaluated
after training. Additionally, the training metrics (F1 and PR-AUC) are
saved on each client for each round to later be plotted and analyzed.
Lastly, the training time and data transfer are logged to evaluate
the computational cost of the federated approach.

For the federated experiments, the same model architecture and training
hyperparameters were used as in the centralized baseline to ensure a fair
comparison. The federated training process was monitored using the same
performance metrics as the centralized baseline, along with the additional
metric of communication overhead (measured in terms of data transferred
between clients and server).

To simulate a realistic federated learning scenario, NVFlare was configured
to run in Proof Of Concept (POC) mode \cite{nvflare_poc_doc}. The POC command
enables a proof-of-concept setup on a single machine. In this configuration,
separate processes emulate the server, clients, and admin console, providing
a practical environment to test and validate workflows before moving to a fully
distributed deployment.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Centralized Baseline}

The paper describing GraphIDS reports a macro F1-score of 0.9447 and
a PR-AUC of 0.8819 on the NF-CSE-CIC-IDS2018-v3 dataset \cite{guerra2025}.
The model ran on a single NVIDIA A100 GPU with 40GB of memory, for our
experiments, the model ran on a single NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPU with 24GB of
memory. The obtained results were a macro F1-score of 0.9517 and
a PR-AUC of 0.8939, which are slightly higher than those reported in the
original paper, which can be attributed to differences in hardware.

The centralized model was trained with both the final subsample of the
dataset, and a non balanced version of the dataset (AD and Others category).
The results are as follows:

\begin{table}[ht]
    \centering
    \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.3}
    \begin{tabular}{|l|c|c|}
        \hline
        \textbf{Metric} & \textbf{Final Subsample} & \textbf{Unbalanced Subsample} \\ \hline
        Macro F1-score  & 0.9205                   & 0.9385                        \\ \hline
        PR-AUC          & 0.6612                   & 0.6471                        \\ \hline
    \end{tabular}
    \caption{Model Performance Comparison Across Subsets}
    \label{tab:performance_comparison}
\end{table}

As shown in \autoref{tab:performance_comparison}, the model trained on the
final subsample achieved a higher PR-AUC but a lower F1-score compared to the
model trained on the unbalanced subsample. This suggests that while the final
subsample may have improved the model's ability to distinguish between classes
(as reflected in the higher PR-AUC), it may have also introduced some noise or
reduced the model's overall accuracy (as reflected in the lower F1-score). The
unbalanced subsample, on the other hand, may have allowed the model to achieve a
higher F1-score by leveraging the class imbalance, but at the cost of a lower
PR-AUC, indicating that the model may have struggled to effectively identify
positive samples in the presence of the imbalance.
